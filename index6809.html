‘What do you do?..’ ‘I am an artist.’

  • by Simon Blundell
  • 1 Comment

When asked ‘what do you do?’ by an interested someone at a party often the answer is ‘I am a..’ and what follows is some kind of role or roles. If your answer is ‘I am a teacher.. a brick layer.. bus driver.. poet..’ it is an indication that that role has become a part of who you are. This maybe just one part of you because you may follow up with ‘.. mother.. brother..’ or ‘chess player’ adding ‘in any spare time I have left’. However the questioner is usually looking for an answer to ‘how do you earn your living?’ 

Stopping to think about your answer you might wonder if it was the right decision to spend your life making a living in this way and give supplementary answers like ‘I have always wanted to be..’ However you might not stop to think and continue to explain where you work or what your working day consists of. 

For 28 years I stood in front of a class, took registers, planned lessons, followed the ever changing political football of education in the UK, exhibited students work, turned up for the same classes at the same time, term after term, until I then turned up to classes at slightly different times – September until July, year after year. Part way through what people called ‘my teaching career’ I started to answer the ‘what do you do?’ question by saying ‘I work as a teacher’ sometimes following up with the preceding list. 

You might not think that there is much difference between ‘I am a teacher’ and ‘I work as a teacher’ but since leaving the job of teaching I have come to realise that there is. Now, no longer working as a teacher, I can’t answer the question as I had done. Now I  skirt around the subject by saying ‘I retired early and my days consist of gardening, some painting of pictures, housework, a little cooking and some writing.’ I could add listening to Russell Brand, reading, yoga, listening to the birds sing and following the sun across the sky. I often don’t mention those things because of a nagging guilt at the thought this is how everybody should be spending their lives. Of course if the system was organised to benefit the many instead of the few more people could live their lives knowing they could look forward to everyday being able personally develop their own projects and live a more fulfilling and contented life.

Now I could answer ‘I am an artist..’ but of course I don’t. You could say that the reason I don’t answer like this is because I don’t make money from this activity (as if that was the most important aspect of life). There are lots of things that fall under the heading ‘Things I do’. As long as those things remain under that heading and not under the heading ‘Simon’s identity’

There are obviously things I might tell myself that I would miss, if suddenly, I could no longer garden or paint or read and write. Having those activities as identities denoted by the words ‘I am..’ would, I think, bring about a crisis in identity. I think I found adapting to no longer doing the job of a teacher relatively easy partly because I never invested much energy into the label.

So now when I am asked I don’t respond ‘I am an artist’. The consequence of relieving myself of this identity is that I only paint when I want to paint (which happens to be some of most days) and don’t feel anxiety about not painting. I might feel put out that a task presents itself that needs taking care of, especially if I am particularly delighting in a painting and wanting to lose myself in it again. Simply if I don’t feel motivated to paint or draw and a book is more engrossing, a walk beckons or the pleasure of gardening or making pumpkin soup promises fulfilment then that is where energy needs to be invested.  Creating artworks is not being employed by an external body but rather an internal energy that creates its own momentum, or not, and in which case the internal energy is harnessed for other uses.

What are more creative questions you could ask at a party?
